The Global Burden of Climate Change

Background

As we as a society look at ways to address the global warming crisis, there is a struggle between scientists pushing for fast and decisive action to save the planet, and policy makers looking to balance short term needs like employment and economic growth. Green alternatives are unfortunately not always the most economical and the economic costs often disproportionately affect more vulnerable groups. Developing countries often bear more of the burden of climate change and are less equipped to adapt. On a global scale, we must determine what nations are currently impacted by the impacts of global warming, and what nations stand to be impacted most in the coming years. What countries are the biggest polluters? What climate change solutions can equitably and positvely affect all nations equitably? How can the burden of economic impacts of these solutions be equitably distributed? A good place to start is by examining trends in climate change globally over the last half a century. The easiest way to see that is to look at average temperatures across the globe and how they have changed in the last fifty years. The interactive map below can be used to examine the change from the average temperature from 1951-1980 to the present.

The above visualization helps to begin to show us who is most impacted, but it is also important to know who is contributing the most to climate change. The chart below visually shows the 10 biggest contributors in average annual carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions by the combustion of coal, natural gas, oil, and other fuels, including industrial waste and non-renewable municipal waste data compiled by the Global Carbon Project. The realization that emerges from this figure is that, in general, developed countries and major emerging economy nations lead in total carbon dioxide emissions.These uneven contributions to the climate crisis are at the core of the challenges the world community faces in finding effective and equitable solutions to global warming. Global Analysis via the GAIN Index

Diving deeper into the environmental inequity between developing and developed nations, we found Notre Dame's Global Adaptation Initiative Index to be an invaluable resource. The GAIN Index aims to assess countries' exposure to climate change as well as their ability to adapt--quantified by a Vulnerability Score and a Readiness Score. The maps below provide a quick overview of GAIN scores from 2019. The map on the left shows the Vulnerability Score for each country, while the map on the right shows the Readiness Score. Vulnerability refers to the potential for negative impact due to climate change, while Readiness encompasses a country's capacity to leverage new technologies to take adaptive action. These scores are calculated using a multitude of indicators across various sectors: health, food, water, governance, energy, technology, economy, and so on. I chose a map to dispaly this data as it is powerful to see how geography plays a role in risk and readiness. By comparing between the two maps, we can see that many of the countries who are the most vulnerable to climate change are also the least ready to combat it--emphasizing the environmental inequity that exists between developed and developing countries, and how critical it is that fighting climate change is a worldwide effort.

To further explore the insights the GAIN index provides, we compared countries' Vulnerability and Readiness scores and studied the trends and outleirs. The data is broken up into four quadrants based on Climate Vulnerability and Readiness. A negative sloping trend is apparent, showcasing that the countries at the highest risk are also the least prepared. Each data point's size is encoded by GDP--it is very clear that smaller GDP, developing countries, are much more adversely impacted by climate change and much less ready. This is a stark representation of the environmental inequity present across the globe that must be addressed as we think about solutions to climate change.

Next, we began to look for relationships between GAIN scores and other metrics. Visualized here are several key metrics: Vulnerability Score (ND-Gain), Readiness Score, and Poverty Level (calculated based on percent of a nation's citizens living below a baseline world income level). The two concerning trends are quite obvious, yet unsurprising. Countries with a higher percentage of the population below the poverty level are far more vulnerable to factors associated with climate change and are far less prepared for climate change. On the left hand graph, points are also color-coded based on their readiness score, emphasizing how nations with high levels of poverty are both more vulnerable and less prepared.

We next used the GAIN index to look at the inbalance between those who will be most impacted by climate change and those who are the main consumers of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and natural gas. To vizualize this, we summed the fossil fuel consumption for individual nations over the past twenty years and evaluated on a scatterplot in comparison to their climate Readiness score. Scaling by population was an option. However, the reality is that the amount of fossil fuels consumed by a country is directly proportional to its contribution of additional CO2 into the atmosphere, rather than a nation's fossil fuel consumption per person. The visualization underscores an uncomfortable issue: countries that are less vulnerable and more prepared are also predominantly resposible for global fossil fuel usage, whereas those who have contributed less to Climate Change, and additional atmopsheric CO2, are more vulnerable and elss prepared.

As we acknowledge global environmental inequity, we must look at solutions. Using the same axis as the previous plot (readiness vs vulnerability), we can analyze a nation's renewable energy production for the year 2019. A similar, but less proncounced trend appears. Less vulnerable and more ready nations utilize renewable energy at a higher level that less prepared and more vulnerable nations. This graphic may not show the entire picture and likely should be scaled by population. Another potentially altering factor was the decision to exclude hydro power from the renewables section. Though hydroelectric damns are techinically a renewable source of energy given that water is renewable relative to fossil fuels. Air Pollution and GDP

It is usually assumed that developing countries struggle with air pollution more than developed countries since they are less likely to invest in cleaner sources of energy because these renewable resources tend to be more expensive to procure than cheap and readily available fossil fuels like coal. To check if that is the case, let us now analyze the global death rates from outdoor air pollution and the GDP per capita for each country to see if there is a strong correlation between these two factors. Here, we constructed a scatterplot to show the relationship between the two quantitative variables being measured and plotted a trendline to act as an approximation of the data points. From our trendline, we see that there is a negative trend. In addition, our R^2 value (coefficient of determination) of 0.014926. The coefficient of determination can be thought of as a percent. It gives us an idea of how many data points fall within the trend line. The higher the coefficient, the higher percentage of points the line passes through when the data points and line are plotted. In this case, only 1.49% of the points fall within our trendline which indicates a very weak to almost nonexistent relationship between a country's GDP per capita and air pollution death rates. Hence, we cannot automatically assume that developing countries are more at risk of the dangers of air pollution, but it is still an issue that they cannot invest in clean energy and must rely on cheap fossil fuels in order for their economies to grow and stabilize.
