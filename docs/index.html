Background

As we as a society look at ways to address the global warming crisis, there is a struggle between scientists pushing for fast and decisive action to save the planet, and policy makers looking to balance short term needs like employment and economic growth. Green alternatives are unfortunately not always the most economical and the economic costs often disproportionately affect more vulnerable groups. Developing countries often bear more of the burden of climate change and are less equipped to adapt. On a global scale, we must determine what nations are currently impacted by the impacts of global warming, and what nations stand to be impacted most in the coming years. What countries are the biggest polluters? What climate change solutions can equitably and positvely affect all nations equitably? How can the burden of economic impacts of these solutions be equitably distributed?

Global Analysis via the GAIN Index

Quick description: This visualization comes from data provided by Notre Dame's Global Adaptation Initiative Index. This index aims to assess countries' exposure to climate change as well as their ability to adapt. The map on the left shows the Vulnerability Score for each country, while the map on the right shows the Readiness Score. Vulnerability refers to the potential for negative impact due to climate change, while Readiness encompasses a country's capacity to leverage new technologies to take adaptive action. These scores are calculated using a multitude of indicators across various sectors: health, food, water, governance, energy, technology, economy, and so on. I chose a map to dispaly this data as it is powerful to see how geography plays a role in risk and readiness. By comparing between the two maps, we can see that many of the countries who are the most vulnerable to climate change are also the least ready to combat it--emphasizing the environmental inequity that exists between developed and developing countries, and how critical it is that fighting climate change is a worldwide effort.

Quick Description: It is usually assumed that developing countries struggle with air pollution more than developed countries since they are less likely to invest in cleaner sources of energy because these renewable resources tend to be more expensive to procure than cheap and readily available fossil fuels like coal. To check if that is the case, let us now analyze the global death rates from outdoor air pollution and the GDP per capita for each country to see if there is a strong correlation between these two factors. Here, we constructed a scatterplot to show the relationship between the two quantitative variables being measured and plotted a trendline to act as an approximation of the data points. From our trendline, we see that there is a negative trend. In addition, our R^2 value (coefficient of determination) of 0.014926. The coefficient of determination can be thought of as a percent. It gives us an idea of how many data points fall within the trend line. The higher the coefficient, the higher percentage of points the line passes through when the data points and line are plotted. In this case, only 1.49% of the points fall within our trendline which indicates a very weak to almost nonexistent relationship between a country's GDP per capita and air pollution death rates. Hence, we cannot automatically assume that developing countries are more at risk of the dangers of air pollution, but it is still an issue that they cannot invest in clean energy and must rely on cheap fossil fuels in order for their economies to grow and stabilize.
